frames¶

The frames file contains methods that can be used to transform back and forth between geodetic and Cartesian coordinates. The methods in this file reley on the us eod Datum dataclasses that are wither created by the user or are selected from pre-existing dataclasses in the geo_py.datum file.

Coordinate Transformations¶

The folloing functions can be used to transform back and forth between LLH (Latitude, Longitude, Height), ECEF (Earth Centered Earth Fixed), ENU (East, North, Up), and NED (North, East, Down) coordinate frames.

geo_py.frames.llh_to_ecef(lat: float, lon: float, alt: float, dat: Datum = WGS84(a=6378137.0, b=6356752.314245, f=0.0033528106647474805, e=0.08181919084262157, e2=0.00669437999014132, GM=398600500000000.0, R_avg=6371008.8, e24=0.006694317779487594, g_avg=9.7803253359)) → Tuple[float, float, float][source]¶
Parameters:

  • lat – The latitude in decimal degrees

  • lon – The longitude in decimal degrees

  • alt – The altitude above sea-level in units of meters

  • datum – A Datum dataclass, defaulted to WGS84

Return x, y, z:

The ECEF coordinates in units of meters

This method will transform a data from a LLH (Latitude, Longitude, Height) coordinate frame to an ECEF (Earth Centered Earth Fixed) coordinate frame. The LLH frame is a polar coordinate frame with its origin at the center of the Earth. The ECEF frame is a Cartesian coordinate system with its origin at the center of the Earth.

This method solves the following equations for \(X\), \(Y\), and \(Z\) assuming inputs of latitude (\(\lambda\)), longitude (\(\phi\)), and altitude (\(h\)). The terms \(a\), \(b\), and \(e\) represents the semi-major axis, the semi-minor axis and the eccentricity of the spheroid, and are derived from the Datum dataclass

\[\begin{split}\begin{eqnarray} X = \left(N\left(\phi\right) + h\right)cos\phi \: cos\lambda \\ Y = \left(N\left(\phi\right) + h\right)cos\phi \: sin\lambda \\ Z = \left(\frac{b^2}{a^2}N\left(\phi\right) + h \right)sin\:\phi \end{eqnarray}\end{split}\]

where

\[N\left(\phi\right) = \frac{a}{\sqrt[]{1-e^2sin^2\:\phi}}\]

from geo_py.frames import llh_to_ecef

lat = 46.826
lon = 107.321
alt = 6096.0
x, y, z = llh_to_ecef(lat, lon, alt)
print(x, y, z)
>>> -1302839.38, 4177542.21, 4632996.83

Return lat, lon, alt:

The latitude, longitude, and altitude in the LLH coordinate frame. Latitude and longitude are in units of decimal degrees, altitude in units of meters

This method will transform a data from an ECEF (Earth Centered Earth Fixed) coordinate frame to a LLH (Latitude, Longitude, Height) coordinate frame. The LLH frame is a polar coordinate frame with its origin at the center of the Earth. The ECEF frame is a Cartesian coordinate system with its origin at the center of the Earth.

This method takes the semi-major and semi-minor variables (\(a\), \(b\)) from the Datum dataclass and use them with the ECEF inputs \(X\), \(Y\), \(Z\) to calculate latitude, longitude, and height \(\phi\), \(\lambda\), \(h\) in accordance with the following equations.

\[\begin{split}\begin{eqnarray} e^2 = \frac{a^2-b^2}{a^2} \\ e^{\prime 2} = \frac{a^2-b^2}{b^2} \\ p = \sqrt[]{X^2+Y^2} \\ F = 54b^2\:Z^2 \\ G = p^2 + \left(1 - e^2\right)Z^2 - e^2 \left(a^2-b^2\right) \\ c = \frac{e^4Fp^2}{G^3} \\ s = \sqrt[3]{1 + c + \sqrt[]{c^2 + 2c}} \\ k = s + 1 + \frac{1}{s} \\ P = \frac{F}{3k^2G^2} \\ Q = \sqrt[]{1+2e^4P} \\ r_o = \frac{-Pe^2p}{1+Q} \\ r_o2 = \frac{1}{2}a\left(1+\frac{1}{Q}\right) \\ r_o3 = -\frac{P\left(1-e^2\right)Z^2}{Q\left(1+Q\right)} - \frac{1}{2}Pp^2 \\ r_o = r_o + \sqrt[]{r_o2 + r_o3} \\ U = \sqrt[]{\left(p-e^2r_o\right)^2+Z^2} \\ V = \sqrt[]{\left(p-e^2r_o\right)^2+\left(1-e^2\right)Z^2} \\ z_o = \frac{b^2Z}{aV} \\ h = U\left(1-\frac{b^2}{aV}\right) \\ \phi = arctan\left[\frac{Z+e^{\prime2}z_o}{p}\right] \\ \lambda = arctan2\left[Y, X\right] \end{eqnarray}\end{split}\]

This method transforms a three dimensional location from an ECEF (Earth Centered Earth Fixed) coordinate frame to an ENU (East, North, Up) coordinate frame. The ENU frame is determined relative to a location, which is the point of interest. An example would be an aircraft as the reference point, and a runway as the point of interest. This method solves the following equation where \(X_p\), \(Y_p\), and \(Z_p\) are the origin point in ECEF coordinates, \(\lambda\), \(\lambda\), and \(\phi\) are the latitude, longitude and altitude of the reference point in decimal degrees and meters. The term \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\) are the NED coordinates in units of meters. In addition \(X_r\), \(Y_r\), and \(Z_r\) are the reference point expressed in ECEF coordinates

\[\begin{split}\begin{bmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -sin\lambda & cos\lambda & 0\\ -sin\phi\:\cos\lambda & -sin\phi\:\sin\lambda & cos\phi\\ cos\phi\:cos\lambda & cos\phi\:sin\lambda & sin\phi \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} X_p-X_r \\ Y_p-Y_r \\ Z_p-Z_r \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

This function converts the ECEF (Earth Centered Earth Fixed) coordinate frame to the NED (North, East, Down) coordinate frame. This function solves the following equations where \(e\) represents the body’s eccentricity, \(a\) represents the bodies semi-major axis length, \(X\), \(Y', and :math:`Z\) represents the ECEF coordinates of the origin point. Finally \(\phi\), \(\lambda\), and \(h\) represent the latitude, longitude and height of the origin point above sea level.

\[\begin{split}\begin{bmatrix} N \\ E \\ D \\ \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -sin\lambda\:cos\phi & sin\lambda\:sin\phi & cos\lambda\\ -sin\phi & cos\phi & 0\\ cos\lambda\:cos\phi & -cos\lambda\:sin\phi & -sin\lambda \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} X-X_r \\ Y-Y_r \\ Z-Z_r \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

This function translates a NED (North, East, Down) coordinate frame to a LLH (Latitude, Longitude, Height) frame. WARNING: The values of origin_lat, origin_lon, and origin_alt should be the same ones used to transform to ENU coordinates

Code Example

from geo_py.frames import ned_to_enu

radar_lat = 46.017
radar_lon = 7.750
radar_alt = 1673.0
N = 28882,283
E = -3552.574
D = 6372030.823

lat, lon, alt = ned_to_enu(radar_lat, radar_lon, radar_alt, N, E, D)
print(lat, lon, alt)
>>> -7134.757, -4556.321, 2852.390

Euler Angles¶

The following functions will conduct passive rotations based on Direction Cosines or Quaternians.

geo_py.frames.pry_to_dcm(pitch: float, roll: float, yaw: float) → ndarray[source]¶
Parameters:

  • pitch – The angle of attack of the craft relative to the x-axis in units of radians.

  • roll – The angle of the craft relative to the y-axis in units of radians

  • yaw – The angle of the craft relative to the z-axis, also the same as heading, in units of radians.

Return dcm:

The direction cosine matrix specific for rotations around the Y, X, and Z axis, or the pitch, roll, and yaw axis.

This function is written to calcualte the Diection Cosine Matrix in the proper order for determining the orientation of a craft with knowledge of its pitch, roll, and yaw. This function returns the matrix of the following format where \(\theta\), \(\phi\), and \(\psi\) represent pitch, roll, and yaw respectively.

\[\begin{split}R_{xyz}= \begin{bmatrix} cos\theta\:cos\psi & cos\theta\:sin\psi & -sin\theta \\ sin\phi\:sin\theta\:cos\psi & sin\phi\:sin\theta\:sin\psi & cos\theta\:sin\phi \\ cos\phi\:sin\theta\:cos\psi+sin\phi\:sin\psi & cos\phi\:sin\theta\:sin\psi-sin\phi\:cos\psi & cos\theta\:cos\phi \\ \end{bmatrix}\end{split}\]

Code Example

from geopy.frames import pry_to_dcm
pitch = 0.1
roll = 0.0
yaw = 0.7854
dcm = pry_to_dcm(pitch, roll, yaw)
print(dcm)
>>> [[ 0.7035729 0.70357548 -0.09983342]
     [-0.70710808 0.70710548 0.]
     [0.07059276 0.07059302 0.9950417]]
geo_py.frames.direction_cosines(vector: ndarray) → Tuple[float, float, float][source]¶
Parameters:

vector – A three dimensional vector

Return cos_x, cos_y, cos_z:

The direction cosines for a vector

This function returns the direction cosines of a vector (\(\alpha\), \(\beta\), \(\gamma\)) via the following equation.

\[\begin{split}v = ai + bj + ck \\ cos\alpha = \frac{a}{\sqrt[]{a^2+b^2+c^2}} \\ cos\beta = \frac{b}{\sqrt[]{a^2+b^2+c^2}} \\ cos\gamma = \frac{c}{\sqrt[]{a^2+b^2+c^2}} \\\end{split}\]

Code Example

from geo_py.frames import direction_cosines
vec = np.array([1., 2., 3.])
cos_x, cos_y, cos_z = direction_cosines(vec)
print(cos_x, cos_y, cos_z)
>>> 0.26726, 0.53452, 0.801783
